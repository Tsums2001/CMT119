Joan Clarke is one of the important figures in breaking the German Enigma ciphers, she was a young mathematician who broke 3 signals in the German Enigma and was only paid £2 a week (Erskine, R., 1997). She was one of the only women to work within the nerve centre and was head of Hut 8, being the longest serving member.

Due to Clarke being a woman of the time she was denied her full double first degree in mathematics from Cambridge, however through her incredible work in the aid to decipher the German Enigma she was able to pave the acceptance for women within the realm of not only mathematics, but also computer science with her working on some of the hardest ciphers to decode.

Clarke and her associates used the core principles of Computational Thinking to aid them in deciphering and cracking the German Enigma codes to turn the tide in World War 2, allowing for new methods to enable them in coding and decoding programs to aid not only the war but earlier computers to help scientists with problem such as finding approximations for thermonuclear and fission devices (Denning, P.J. and Tedre, M., 2021).

Clarke came from an era where women were not appreciated for intelligence and this maybe why we hear quite little of anything else she did within the realms of computer science and mathematics. She’s an important figure in computer science because it allowed a field once completely dominated by men, to usher in an acceptance for women within STEM fields to further push for progress in all academic fields essentially double the potential of people that could create groundbreaking research in their respected fields.

Allowing for other renown female computer scientists, such as Cecili R. Aragon (an American computer scientist who co-developed one of known binary search trees called treap data structure), and Elizabeth Feinler (an American computer scientist that managed ARPANET and DDN, two of the biggest networks that eventually become the foundation for the world wide web).
